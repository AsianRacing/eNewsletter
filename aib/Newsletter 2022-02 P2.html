Dear all,

Welcome to the newsletter produced by the ARF Council on Anti-Illegal Betting & Related Financial Crime. The newsletter is bi-weekly, to provide a more timely overview of the situation relating to illegal betting and related financial crime.
 
Illegal betting continues to generate major news stories in every region. In Hong Kong, there were reports that the police have arrested several online “influencers” for promoting illegal betting. They were giving reviews of illegal betting websites to provide to their Instagram followers, for which they were paid HKD 3,000 each time, which could generate income of HKD 100,000 per month.
 
These cases follow the report by ARF Council members Doug Robinson and James Porteous on ‘Social media advertising of illegal betting in Hong Kong’ in the Quarterly Bulletin published in January in which they stated that “Since late 2019, ‘spam’ advertising of illegal betting in Hong Kong via social media apps has become ubiquitous, with unsolicited messages being sent on messaging applications such as WhatsApp, Telegram and Signal, and via platforms such as Facebook, Instagram and Twitter.”
 
The crackdown on cross-border illegal betting and gambling by authorities in the region has continued. In Cambodia, the telecommunications regulator has blocked 15 illegal online lottery websites, which the authorities stated have a harmful impact on the poorest in society. In China, the Minister of Public Security made a statement saying that in 2022 there will be a centralised nationwide effort against cross-border gambling with “cluster strikes against major cases”, as well as improvement of a blacklist of overseas tourism destinations luring Chinese citizens for gambling purposes.
 
In the USA, the legalisation of online sports betting by many states has had an impact on the huge illegal betting market, illustrated by betting on the recent NFL Superbowl game which the American Gaming Association predicted would attract USD7.6 billion in bets, although this included bets made with illegal bookmakers.
 
The situation in Asia shows why it is important for the authorities to recognise the transnational organised crime nature of illegal betting, while developments in the USA illustrate how a structured and regulated legal betting market can undermine the illegal market.
 
I hope that this newsletter gives you some insight into the continued global problem of illegal betting and its negative impact.


Best wishes,
Martin Purbrick
Chairperson, Asian Racing Federation Council on Anti-illegal Betting & Related Financial Crime

Cambodia Shuts Another 15 Illegal Gambling Websites
Gambling News, 17 February 2022

Cambodia continues to double down on its efforts of keeping the online gambling industry clean. To this end, the country’s telecommunications regulator has been able to suspend access to 15 illegal online lottery websites. Cambodia stopped issuing new online gambling licenses on January 1, 2020, making it clear that interactive gambling would have no place in the country.

In November last year, Cambodia shut down 79 illegal gambling websites, arguing that they posed dangers to public security. Since then, authorities have only intensified their bid to shut down such operations.
Cambodia has also been criticised for the ongoing human trafficking in illegal casinos
China vows to accelerate fight against cross-border gambling
Inside Asian Gaming, 17 February 2022

China’s Ministry of Public Security has vowed to continue the nation’s fight against cross-border gambling, issuing a strongly worded statement following a special meeting on the subject this week. The Ministry said it would “continue to advance the work of combating cross-border gambling in depth, resolutely curbing cross-border gambling chaos and striving to create a safe and stable political and social environment for the successful convening of the 20th National Congress of the Communist Party of China,” which is set to take place in the northern hemisphere autumn of 2022.

In particular, the Ministry outlined a plan to “completely destroy” networks of foreign “mega-gambling groups”, seemingly a reference to Macau’s junkets. The statement cited China’s success in shutting down such cross-border gambling groups in 2021, referencing the destruction of “A Group of extraordinarily large cross-border gambling gangs” which it said had been recruiting citizens for gambling purposes and sucking money out of China. This appears to be a reference to Suncity Group, which shut down its junket business in December following the arrest and detention of its CEO, Alvin Chau.

According to the Ministry, 2022 will see a centralised nationwide effort against cross-border gambling with “cluster strikes against major cases”. It also promised to improve a previously announced blacklist of overseas tourism destinations said to be luring Chinese citizens for gambling purposes.

“It is necessary to thoroughly identify those who go abroad to be involved in gambling fraud, improve the entry and exit ‘blacklist’ of gambling-related personnel, dynamically adjust the ‘blacklist’ of tourist destinations, continue to strengthen the construction of border material defense technology and carry out in-depth efforts to crack down on obstructing national border management,” the statement said.

ACMA study shows increase in Australians using online gambling services during COVID
Inside Asian Gaming, 18 February 2022

A new study conducted by the Australian Communications and Media Authority (ACMA) has revealed an increase in the number of Australians gambling online over the six month period through to June 2021.

The study found that more than one in 10 (11%) Australians reported participating in online gambling at some stage in the previous six months, up from 8% in 2020. According to ACMA, it also highlights significant growth in online sports betting since the 2020 survey, likely driven by the return of sporting events after the COVID-related lockdowns of that year.

Online sports and race betting – both legal in Australia – were “marginally more popular” than online gaming activities like poker machines, poker or casino-style table games, which are all illegal. The research also showed that a small proportion of Australian adults are using illegal online wagering services with one in 20 online gamblers reporting the use of an offshore betting platform.

BGC issues stark warning after use of black market sites found to have doubled gambling
Racing Post, 18 February 2022

As it prepares its long-awaited white paper on gambling, the British government was urged to learn lessons from abroad after new research revealed the number of British punters using black market betting sites has doubled in the last two years.
The Betting and Gaming Council pointed to a huge increase in illegal betting following the introduction of strict new measures on regulated operators.

The Charlotte Observer, 19 February 2022

Among the more nettlesome problems facing the U.S. legalised sports betting industry, especially the online version, is the challenge posed by offshore internet sportsbooks. Most stakeholders concede that the offshore websites still have the majority of the digital sports betting market. And so far, there’s been no substantially effective counter-strategy to stop bettors from going to a gambling marketplace where they may feel they’re getting better odds and can more easily evade individual taxes, albeit without any consumer protections and with the knowledge that they are supporting an illicit market.

The Asian Racing Federation Council on Anti-illegal Betting and Related Financial Crime

The Asian Racing Federation Council on Anti-illegal Betting and Related Financial Crime was established in 2017 as a think tank aimed at combatting illegal betting and related financial crime. The ARF Council now comprises 15 members from organisations engaged in horse racing and sports integrity, law enforcement, the United Nations, and academia.

The ARF Council’s purpose is to research and share the scale and negative impacts of illegal betting, particularly as it relates to horse racing and sports integrity, and to foster international collaboration among stakeholders, such as horse racing operators and authorities, gambling regulators, law enforcement agencies, and government policy makers to raise awareness of the threat and to combat the negative impact of illegal betting and other financial crimes to horse racing, other sports, and to society.
